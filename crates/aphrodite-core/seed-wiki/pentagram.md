# Pentagram — why it matters

Pentagram's site is the gold standard for "a design studio showing its own work." Each project page is a long-scroll case study with the *finished identity system* shown at scale across applications (stationery, signage, packaging, web). The page chrome (nav, footer) is whisper-quiet so the work can be loud.

## What to absorb

- **Project hero = one large image of the *finished work in context*** — not a hero of the studio, not a hero of a logo on white. Real-world application: signage at scale, packaging in hand, a printed piece on a desk.
- **Case-study scroll structure.** Hero → 1-paragraph problem statement → 8-15 images of the solution at various scales → credits at the foot. No bullet-pointed "key features."
- **Sans-serif everywhere** (currently NB International Pro), regular and medium weights only. No serif display for the studio's own brand — the studio is a vessel; the work is the content.
- **Caption-driven.** Each image has a short, factual caption: "Wordmark, applied to entrance signage, 240 × 60 cm." Not "An impactful brand expression that captures the essence of…"
- **Grid: 12-column with 24 px gutter.** Each project image lands on column boundaries, never floats mid-column.

## What NOT to copy

- Pentagram's own brand restraint *only* works because they have decades of celebrated work to point at. A new studio adopting the same restraint without the work behind it reads as empty.
- Don't copy the *partner-author byline* format unless your studio actually credits partner-led projects.

## Reference fragments worth lifting

- Project nav: 4 partners' names in a horizontal strip at top — a quietly chest-thumping move that names the people, not just the studio
- Caption sizing: 11 px caps, letterspacing 0.05em, slate grey on near-white
- Footer: project credits list (designers, photographers, year) in a single dense paragraph, not in a styled "Team" section
