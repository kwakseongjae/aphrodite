# Apartamento — why it matters

Apartamento has been the reference editorial site for "people-and-their-spaces" content since 2008. The web translation of the print magazine keeps the *pacing* of print: each story is a vertical scroll where photographs occupy generous space and the text captions are narrow, intimate, and short. No "above-the-fold" optimization. No CTA stack.

## What to absorb

- **Editorial pacing.** Each "row" of content should feel like a magazine spread — one dominant image, narrow text rail to one side. Not a card grid.
- **Photography at 4:5 or 5:6 portrait ratio.** Not 16:9. Portrait orientation respects the *subject*, which is usually a person or an object in a space.
- **Display serif (typically Caslon or Plantin) paired with a light grotesque sans for captions.** The display carries weight; the sans recedes.
- **Generous side margins.** On a 1440 viewport, the content column is often 50-60% of width. The rest is intentional emptiness.
- **Article title in oversized display (60-96 px). Body in 15-17 px Caslon or Plantin equivalent.**
- **Short, intimate captions.** "Hakwi dining table, black walnut, 2024" not "This stunning piece of contemporary craftsmanship represents…"

## What NOT to copy

- Don't replicate the *byline-and-publication-date stamp* unless the surface is editorial. A maker's portfolio is not a magazine — it's a body of work; the byline is implicit.
- Don't add Apartamento's specific photography filters or grain. Their photography is *commissioned* with that look; faking it via CSS produces uncanny stock-photo vibes.

## Reference fragments worth lifting

- Article header: title in serif display 80 px, byline in caps 11 px below, then a 400 px space, then the photograph
- Photo width: 60% of column, never full-bleed unless deliberate
- Caption: 13 px italic, 80% opacity, left-aligned to photograph
- Footer: a single line of small caps, centered, with a contact email
