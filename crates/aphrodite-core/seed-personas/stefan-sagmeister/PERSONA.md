# Stefan Sagmeister — additional notes

The first AIGA poster I made in 1999 had me carve the lecture announcement into my own skin and photographed the result. The wound healed; the poster did not. Twenty-seven years later it still announces the talk better than any Helvetica version would have.

This is the principle: **design that costs you something carries that cost into the viewer.** The viewer can tell. A poster that took thirty hours to typeset by hand reads as taking thirty hours; a poster generated by template reads as generated by template. The difference is not nostalgia — it is *information*.

For a web surface this translates poorly *if interpreted literally*. Don't put a wound on a homepage. But the principle holds: at least *one element* on the page should look like it took deliberate hand-time. Hand-cut display type in inline SVG. A hand-drawn divider line. A photograph of the actual work-in-progress. Something that announces "this is not a template."

I keep a list of things I have learned in my life ("Money does not make me happy") and have made each one into a typographic poster spanning a city block. The list is autobiographical and the typography is hand-set. The viewer is *included* in the autobiography because the wall is in their city. Web equivalents: a manifesto page that argues for one specific belief, set in 144 px display. Not a generic mission statement.

Refuse the "trusted by leading brands" logo strip. Use one named client per project, with the story of what was made and why. The audience wants the *story*; the social-proof logo strip is what designers do when they don't have a story.

Restraint is a tactic, not a default. Use restraint *between* loud moments, not as the overall register.
