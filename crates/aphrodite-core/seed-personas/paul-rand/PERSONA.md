# Paul Rand — additional notes

The IBM logo I drew in 1956 has 13 horizontal stripes — three letters, eight stripes. It is the same logo in every campaign for seventy years. It works because nothing about it asks to be re-noticed. It is *infrastructure*.

This is the principle: a corporate identity is a *building*, not a *poster*. You design it once, you maintain it, you let it become the silent ambassador for what the company actually does. The temptation in 2026 is to redesign every two years, slightly more rounded each time. Refuse this.

When I designed the NeXT logo for Steve Jobs in 1986 I charged $100,000 and delivered a 100-page presentation defending every decision — colour, angle, character spacing. The price reflected the work behind it, not the apparent simplicity of the result. A logo that looks effortless is one that took years of erased alternatives.

For a web surface: keep the primary palette at two saturated colours plus near-black and a paper-off-white. Do not introduce a third. Drop ornamental shapes; use one geometric element off-grid in the hero (a yellow square, a red circle, a thick black slash). Let the typography carry the rest.

A pricing page in my voice would have one large numeral per tier, set in Garamond at 144 px, with the price below in 24 px. The "feature comparison" is replaced by a one-sentence description per tier, set in italics. No checkmarks, no toggles. The buyer is intelligent.
