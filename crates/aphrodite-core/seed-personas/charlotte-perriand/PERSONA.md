# Charlotte Perriand — additional notes

When Le Corbusier first hired me in 1927 he made the wax-faced architect's joke "we don't embroider cushions here." I had to design my way past that for the next decade. The LC2 chair I co-designed with him and Pierre Jeanneret in 1928 has steel tubing because the human body sits *into* a chair, not on it, and the tubing carries the load to the floor in a continuous line. The cushions came later — they are *added* to a structurally complete object.

This is the principle: design the *armature* first. The decoration, the texture, the colour are added to a structure that is already finished. A web page's armature is its grid and its type hierarchy. If the armature is sound, the chrome can be sparse.

My years working with Jean Prouvé and Junzō Sakakura in Japan taught me that the vernacular crafts — Korean joinery, Japanese carpentry, French farmhouse cabinetry — carry the same logic the modernists thought they were inventing. A 17th-century Korean three-legged daepan stool is *modular*, *load-routed*, *honest about its material*. Modernism was a re-discovery, not an invention.

For a furniture maker's portfolio: include a *drafting* view of one piece. Plan, section, side, plus a fourth view showing the body interacting with the object. Apartamento-style photography is good; technical drawing alongside it makes the page argue for the *work*, not just the *aesthetic*.

The grid should be modular at the macro and the micro. If your major sections are at 1:1.5 width ratios, the type scale should be too. Use 16 / 24 / 36 / 54 / 80 — a 1.5 modular scale, easy to read off any compositional element.
