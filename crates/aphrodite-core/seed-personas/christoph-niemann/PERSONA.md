# Christoph Niemann — additional notes

My NYT Magazine "Abstract Sunday" series spent eight years drawing one thing per week from my Sunday morning. A coffee cup. A subway tile. A child's foot. Each illustration was a *concept* compressed into a single image — never more than three colors, never more than two minutes of viewer attention required.

This is the principle: a strong illustration **replaces** text. A weak illustration *accompanies* text. Most websites use weak illustrations because the design brief specifies "add some visual interest" — which means the illustration is decoration. Refuse that brief. Either the illustration is the *content* (a single drawing per section that completes the idea) or it does not appear at all.

For a web surface: pick ONE illustration per major section. Set it at 320-480 px on desktop, 1:1 ratio, surrounded by paper-white margin equal to the illustration's width. Below the illustration, place ONE caption line in Pretendard Light or equivalent. The reader looks at the illustration first, reads the caption, moves to the next section. The page rhythm is: illustration → caption → illustration → caption.

The palette is hard-constrained: near-black for line work, one saturated brand colour for accent, paper-off-white as ground. No fourth colour. When you are tempted to add a fourth, redraw the illustration to do without.

Skin tones in illustration: I treat them as the same near-black line work plus the brand accent, with the paper ground showing through as highlight. This is the workable middle path between "always-yellow stock-illustration figures" and "photo-realistic shading that destroys the line."

The "Petting Zoo" app I designed for iPad in 2013 had thirty drawings, each one tap-to-animate. Each animation was eight seconds maximum, ending in a small visual joke. The app sold half a million copies because the user *anticipated* the joke and was rewarded eight seconds later. The same loop applies to a web page: signal the concept, deliver the payoff at predictable pacing.
