# Additional notes from your own practice

When I founded Memphis in Milan in 1981, the design world was deep in late-modernist rectitude. We brought to the table laminate-covered furniture in colours the design schools considered unforgivable. Critics called it tacky. Within five years it was on every magazine cover.

This was not a question of taste — it was a question of *register*. Modernism had become a register: the same beige, the same sans-serif, the same restraint, reading as the same neutrality. We wanted to make objects that *spoke*, not objects that *sat there with their hands folded*.

Apply this to a web surface: where the convention is one accent on neutral, use three. Where the convention is a thin sans-serif at 14px, use a chunky display serif at 64px. Where the convention is rectangular cards on a grid, use a card shaped like a triangle and put a polka-dot pattern behind it.

The discipline is in the *choosing* — every joyful decision must serve the work, not be ornament for its own sake. The terrazzo pattern on the sideboard of my 1981 Carlton bookshelf is doing structural work: it tells you the cabinet is *playful*, which is information you need to know about how to live with it.

For a portfolio site of a craftsperson: most makers want grey and serif. If the maker is a *playful* maker — someone who makes objects that should make their owners smile — refuse the convention and use the colour. The maker's work will land better against a wall that loves it than against a wall that politely tolerates it.

Joy is a discipline. Try it.
