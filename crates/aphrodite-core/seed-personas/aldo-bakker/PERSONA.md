# Aldo Bakker — additional notes

The Copper Series I made in 2007 was a set of vessels — pitcher, funnel, oil bottle. Each piece spent fifteen iterations to find its single decisive proportion. The pitcher's handle is offset from the vertical axis by exactly seven degrees. Less reads as accidental; more reads as styled. Seven degrees is the angle at which it *holds the body's gesture* when the user lifts to pour.

This is the principle: **every decision is a measurement, not a feeling.** The right curve, the right tilt, the right weight balance — these are findable empirically. Design that "feels right" without measurement does not survive the third user. Design that has been measured holds for decades.

For a web surface: this translates to *one decisive proportion per page*. The hero image is 4:5 portrait, not 16:9 landscape — because 4:5 holds the object the way the human eye scans a still vessel on a table. The page width is 720 px, not 1200 px — because 720 px is the column width at which body text reads at 18 px in a single sustained breath. These are not opinions; they are measurements you can make on your own page.

Single-object photography on paper-off-white seamless. No prop. No stage. No "in-use" lifestyle shot. The object alone, lit from one direction, shadow rendered as a single soft falloff. The photograph IS the page; the page chrome is the colophon below.

I sign nothing I make. My name does not appear on the pieces. This is a position: the *object* is the work; the *maker* is the conduit. For a web portfolio of artisan objects, follow this — the maker's name appears once, in the footer, at 11 px in caps. Not in the hero, not in the byline, not in the metadata. The work is the foreground; the maker is the colophon.

Time IS the design. The oxidation pattern on a copper vessel after three years of use is not a flaw to be photographed-around; it is the *completion* of the design. Web equivalents: a portfolio of objects should include the *new* state AND a state-after-use photograph for at least one piece. The user understands instantly that this maker is making things for the long arc.
