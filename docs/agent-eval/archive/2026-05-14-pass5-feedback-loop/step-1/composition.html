Cellular Senescence and the Architecture of a Longer Life

Dr. Emilia Marchetti Director of Cellular Biology, Ara Institute 12 June 2025

Understanding the Bodies We Inhabit

At the Ara Institute, we study the fundamental mechanisms of ageing — not to chase immortality, but to understand how cellular processes shape the trajectory of human health across decades.

There is a moment in the life of every cell when it must decide whether to continue dividing or to stop. For most of human history, we had no language for this moment. We called it ageing, as if it were a single phenomenon rather than a cascade of trillions of individual decisions made inside every organ, every tissue, every microscopic structure that sustains us. Today, thanks to advances in molecular biology, we have not only a language but an emerging map. And at the centre of that map lies a process called cellular senescence.

Senescence is not simply cell death. A senescent cell remains metabolically active — it continues to consume resources, secrete proteins, and communicate with its neighbours. What changes is its refusal to divide further. Triggered by DNA damage, telomere shortening, or oxidative stress, the cell enters a permanent state of cell-cycle arrest. In a young body, the immune system clears these cells efficiently. In an older one, they accumulate, quietly reshaping the tissue environment around them through what researchers call the senescence-associated secretory phenotype, or SASP.

Key Terminology

SASP — Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype: the cocktail of inflammatory cytokines, growth factors, and proteases that senescent cells secrete, altering neighbouring cell behaviour.

The SASP Paradox

One of the most counterintuitive findings in recent ageing research is that senescent cells are not uniformly harmful. In fact, they play essential roles in wound healing, embryonic development, and tumour suppression. The SASP, for all its inflammatory reputation, can signal the immune system to repair damaged tissue. The problem arises not from the existence of senescent cells, but from their persistence. When clearance fails, the chronic low-grade inflammation they produce — sometimes called inflammageing — becomes a driver of virtually every age-related disease studied in the last two decades.

Senolytics: A New Class of Intervention

The discovery that selectively eliminating senescent cells could improve health outcomes in mice — published first by the Mayo Clinic in 2011 and reproduced dozens of times since — opened a door that had been firmly shut. These interventions, called senolytics, work not by addressing the root causes of senescence but by targeting the survival pathways that allow senescent cells to resist natural apoptosis. The most studied combination, dasatinib plus quercetin, has shown measurable improvements in physical function, grip strength, and exercise capacity in early-phase human trials.

At the Ara Institute, our clinical programmes build on these foundations. Rather than asking whether senolytics can extend maximum lifespan — a question that requires decades to answer definitively — we focus on healthspan: the period of life during which an individual remains free from chronic disease and functional decline. This distinction matters. A therapy that compresses morbidity into the final months of life, even if it does not alter the age at death, represents a profound improvement in human welfare.

"We are not in the business of adding years to life. We are in the business of adding life to years — and we now have molecular tools precise enough to attempt it responsibly."
— Professor Henrik Sørensen, Chief Medical Officer, Ara Institute

Beyond Clearance: Reprogramming the Aged Cell

An alternative to killing senescent cells is reprogramming them — coaxing them back toward a more youthful state without restoring full proliferative capacity (and therefore without increasing cancer risk). This approach, rooted in Shinya Yamanaka's Nobel Prize-winning discovery of induced pluripotent stem cells in 2006, has matured considerably in the past five years. Partial reprogramming, delivered through transient expression of a subset of Yamanaka factors, has reversed hallmarks of ageing in mouse models without triggering teratoma formation.

The challenge now is delivery specificity. Ageing is systemic, but blanket reprogramming is dangerous. The cell that lines your aorta requires different epigenetic instructions than the neuron in your hippocampus, yet both accumulate similar molecular damage over time. Our research group at Ara has spent the last three years developing tissue-targeted delivery vehicles — lipid nanoparticles that preferentially accumulate in liver, muscle, or brain tissue — to ensure that reprogramming signals reach only the cells equipped to respond appropriately.

The Inflammageing Hypothesis

Underlying much of this work is a unifying framework: the inflammageing hypothesis. First articulated by Claudio Franceschi and colleagues in 2000, it proposes that the ageing process is driven, at least in part, by a chronic, sterile, low-grade inflammatory state that increases with age. This inflammation is not the acute response to infection that we recognise as fever and swelling. It is subtler — a continuous background signal produced by accumulated senescent cells, altered gut permeability, immune cell dysregulation, and the gradual failure of clearance mechanisms throughout the body.

Measuring inflammageing has proven remarkably difficult because the signal is diffuse. There is no single biomarker that captures it. Instead, our clinic uses a panel approach: interleukin-6, tumour necrosis factor alpha, C-reactive protein, and a suite of less conventional markers including cellular senescence-associated secretory proteins measured in plasma. By tracking these longitudinally across our patient cohort, we can observe the trajectory of inflammageing over time — and, critically, we can measure whether interventions shift that trajectory downward.

This longitudinal approach to measurement is, we believe, the future of longevity medicine. Single time-point biomarker snapshots tell you where you are. Only repeated measurement tells you where you are going. And in a field defined by interventions whose effects may take years to become apparent, understanding trajectory is not a luxury — it is the minimum standard of care.
