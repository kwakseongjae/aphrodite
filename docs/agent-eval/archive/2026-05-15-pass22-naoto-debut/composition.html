Pull to listen.

Morning — cord pulled, power on.

The radio is housed in a single piece of warm-toned ABS resin, matte-finished, with no visible seams. The speaker grille is a series of horizontal slots cut directly into the face — no cloth, no mesh, no additional part. Sound passes through the same surface you see. The tuning dial turns with a soft detent at each marked frequency. The volume ring surrounds it, concentric, so that one finger adjusts volume while another tunes. The pull cord is sheathed in woven cotton, the same material used in Japanese noren curtains. It ages. It softens. The tassel at its end is knotted by hand in our workshop.

Night — cord at rest, power off.

The radio receives FM and AM. It receives nothing else. There is no Bluetooth, no streaming, no clock display, no preset buttons. The frequencies you hear are the ones in the air around you. This is a choice, not a limitation. A radio that receives everything would be a computer. This is not a computer. It is a tool for listening to the radio, and the radio is still good.
